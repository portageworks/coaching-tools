# Bullets — The Standard
*v1.0 — LOCKED. Operates under the Resume Strategist Charter (docs/resume_strategist_charter.md). This is the rubric for writing and judging experience bullets; it is the source for the bullet rules and examples fed into the resume prompts. Amend deliberately.*

## The one job of a bullet
A bullet exists to make a hiring manager think *"I want this person doing that here."* It is not a record of what you were responsible for. It is evidence of what changed because you showed up.

## Who the bullet is written for
**The hiring manager — the person who can actually say yes.** The bullet's substance, proof, and "so what?" are aimed at them, because they're the one who decides. But the bullet passes through a recruiter/ATS screen *first*, so it must stay legible to a smart non-expert: no impenetrable acronym soup, scale stated in plain terms. The resolution: **substance for the decision-maker, clarity for the gatekeeper.** Keep one concrete layer of domain detail for credibility, wrapped in an outcome anyone can grasp — don't strip the signal to please the screener, and don't bury it where only three specialists could find it.
- ❌ (jargon wall) Implemented an MCP server with Kerberos/SAML SSO over a LiteLLM gateway to optimize corporate token budgets.
- ✅ Built the internal AI integration a dozen engineers adopted on their own, cutting hours of daily copy-paste between Jira and their AI assistant — over secure corporate SSO.

## The universal test: "so what?"
Every bullet must survive one question, asked out loud: **so what?** If the bullet describes something you were assigned, maintained, or participated in, and the reader could shrug, it fails. The answer to "so what?" does **not** require a hard number — a qualitative outcome a hiring manager would actually care about passes. Metrics are welcome when real and defensible; they are never the price of entry.

## The shape: Context / Action / Result (compressed)
Every strong bullet carries three beats, even when they're folded into one line:
- **Context** — the situation, stakes, or problem (often implied, not stated).
- **Action** — what *you specifically* did, led with a strong past-tense verb.
- **Result** — what changed: the outcome, the scale, the "so what?"

You won't always write them in that order — often the result leads — but all three must be present or implied.

## The rules (each with a good/bad pair)

**1. Lead with a strong, specific past-tense verb.**
Ban these openers outright: *Responsible for, Tasked with, Duties included, Helped, Assisted, Worked on, Participated in, Involved in, Supported.* They announce a duty, not an act.
- ❌ Responsible for managing vendor relationships.
- ✅ Renegotiated 12 vendor contracts, cutting annual spend by $1.4M.

**2. End on a result — answer "so what?" (qualitative is fine).**
- ❌ Built a dashboard tracking inventory across stores.
- ✅ (quant) Built an inventory dashboard that surfaced $800K in mislabeled stock and ended a recurring audit finding.
- ✅ (qual) Built the inventory dashboard the operations team now runs every weekly review from.

**3. Quantify when the number is real and defensible — never invent one.**
A true qualitative result beats a fabricated metric every time. If you can't defend the number in the interview, it doesn't go on the page.
- ❌ Improved operational efficiency by 35%. (from what baseline? defensible?)
- ✅ Cut the monthly financial close from a three-person, week-long scramble to a one-day automated run.

**4. One idea per bullet.** No "and also" stacking — it buries both ideas.
- ❌ Led the platform migration and managed the support team and rewrote the onboarding docs.
- ✅ Led the platform migration off legacy infrastructure with zero customer downtime.

**5. Take the credit you earned — not the team's, not none of it.**
"We" erases you; inflation lies. Name your specific scope honestly.
- ❌ Helped the team launch the new payments platform.
- ✅ Owned the authentication layer for the payments launch, integrating SSO across 70,000 endpoints.

**6. Front-load the payload.** The outcome or the strongest element comes first; context trails.
- ❌ In order to reduce churn, after analyzing six months of data, implemented a new onboarding flow that lowered cancellations.
- ✅ Cut first-90-day churn 22% with a redesigned onboarding flow, after tracing the drop to a single broken step.

**7. Specifics over abstractions.** Name the system, the scale, the domain. Kill buzzword filler.
- ❌ Leveraged cross-functional synergies to drive transformational outcomes.
- ✅ Consolidated three point-of-sale data domains into one auditable model across 4,600 stores.

**8. Cut the table stakes.** Don't spend a bullet on what everyone in the role obviously does.
- ❌ Attended team meetings and provided status updates to stakeholders.
- ✅ (delete it — this is not evidence of anything)

**9. Order bullets by impact.** Within each role, the biggest, most thesis-relevant bullet goes first. The reader may not reach bullet four.

**10. The thesis governs (from the charter).** An off-thesis bullet is cut no matter how strong — a beautiful line pointing at the wrong identity muddies the eight-second read.

**11. Length and rhythm.** One to two lines each. No bullet that wraps to leave a single orphan word. Vary the opening verb — three bullets starting "Led" reads like a template.

## The "so what?" ladder (the core technique)
This is how you turn a duty into evidence. Keep asking "so what?" until you hit something a hiring manager cares about.
- **v1 (duty):** Responsible for the company's social media accounts.
- *so what?* →
- **v2 (activity):** Managed all social media accounts, posting daily across platforms.
- *so what?* →
- **v3 (result):** Grew the company's LinkedIn following from 2,000 to 40,000 in a year, making it the top source of inbound demo requests.

Same job. v1 is a duty, v3 is a hire-this-person bullet. The ladder is the work.

## Anti-patterns (name them on sight)
- **The duty list:** "Responsible for X, Y, and Z." Responsibilities, not accomplishments.
- **Responsibility soup:** a paragraph of everything the role touched, no result anywhere.
- **The buzzword bullet:** synergy, transformation, leverage, ecosystem — abstraction carrying the sentence.
- **The humble bullet:** "Assisted with," "Helped to," "Supported the." Self-erasure.
- **The run-on:** "and also… and additionally…" — two weak ideas instead of one strong one.
- **Metric theater:** a big number with no meaning, no baseline, or no defensibility.
